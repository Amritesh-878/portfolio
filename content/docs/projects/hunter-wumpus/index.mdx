## What it is

Hunt the Wumpus, inverted. On a 10×10 grid you search for gold, or the Wumpus itself,
through fog of war, reading sensory hints: a **Breeze** near pits, a **Stench** near the
Wumpus, a **Shine** near gold. The twist is the monster. It isn't scripted; it's a
**PPO-trained reinforcement-learning agent** that hunts _you_, tracking your position from
stale sensory cues rather than direct sight. Four ways it ends: you find the gold, you shoot
the Wumpus (one arrow, cardinal directions only), you fall in a pit, or it devours you.

## Architecture

A **React + Vite** frontend talks to a **FastAPI** backend over REST (`/start`, `/move`). The
backend owns the game engine and delegates the enemy's turn to the PPO agent:

- **Client:** the grid, tiles, HUD, and a `useControls` hook for keyboard input; renders
  fog-of-war state returned by the server.
- **Server:** a core engine validates and applies moves, then asks the Wumpus agent for its
  next action.
- **Agent:** a `stable-baselines3` PPO policy trained in a Gymnasium-compatible environment,
  one per difficulty tier.

## The research

This became a published paper, _Reinforcement Learning for Constraint Satisfaction Game
Agents_, co-authored with Gagan Venkat
([DOI 10.5281/zenodo.20076630](https://doi.org/10.5281/zenodo.20076630)). It works up from
tabular methods, through deep Q-networks, to the adversarial PPO agent. More on the
[research page](/research).

## Design notes

- **Memory-based tracking over line-of-sight.** The Wumpus infers where you are from decaying
  sensory cues, so it reads as _hunting_ rather than teleporting to your tile.
- **Reward shaping** to coax genuine pursuit behaviour without degenerate strategies.
- **Symmetric partial information.** Fog of war on the client keeps the human on the same
  footing as the agent, so neither side sees the whole board.

## What I'd do differently

Training an agent to be _fun to lose to_ is a different objective than training it to win. The
interesting work lived in the reward function and the sensory model, not the algorithm, and
that's where I'd invest more next time.
